About SG's Public buses

Singapore's modern public bus system is run by 4 operators - SBS Transit, SMRT, Tower Transit and Go-Ahead Singapore. All of the public bus system is owned and designed by LTA under the Bus Contracting Model(BCM) which is why newer buses are now under a unified Lush Green livery. The 4 different operators bid for bus route packages to operate the bus services under each package and will be paid service fees with bonuses and penalties depending on their performance.

The roles of bus operators under the BCM is to operate services under their packages with specified performance standards, maintain their bus fleet, depots and interchanges, collect fares on behalf of the governmen and provide bus service information and customer management services.

Why the Bus Contracting Model?

The BCM allows the 4 operators in SG to focus more on operations and meeting service standards and allows LTA to respond quickly to any changes in service demands. It also allows for market competition which encourages operators to provide better bus services for commuters - through more reliable bus services with less waiting time between buses. This contract model is also seen in places like London and certain Australian cities.

Types of Public Bus services in SG

  • Basic Bus Services

    • Basic Bus Services

    Basic bus services in SG consists of Trunk and Feeder services. Trunk services are long bus routes that connect multiple towns across SG, or connect multiple towns with the city area. Feeder services on the other hand, operate around neighbourhoods in a town to connect to various parts of housing estates with bus interchanges and MRT stations.

  • City Direct Services

    • City Direct Services

    City Direct services in SG are bus routes that connect residential estates with the Central Business District(CBD). These services operate only during peak hours and have set boarding and alighting points - passengers can only board at residential stops in the morning and CBD stops in the evening and can only alight at residential stops in the evening and CBD stops in the morning. These services charge fares similar to that of express services.

  • Express Services

    • Express Services

    Express bus services are services that have express sectors, either by skipping bus stops or travelling on expressways. This saves travel time compared to taking regular trunk services. However, they do charge higher fares due to the added convenience. Express services can be classified in 3 groups - Full Day, Limited-Stop and CBD express services. Full Day express services connects residential areas to the city or between towns, Limited-stop express services follow their parent route but stops at fewer bus stops on the route and CBD express services are similar to City Direct services, except that there are no set boarding and alighting points and passengers are free to board or alight at any stop along the route.

  • Premium Services

    • Premium Services

    Premium services are similar to City Direct services by connecting residential areas with work areas like the CBD, business parks and industrial areas. However, they use private coach buses and are charged a flat fare ranging from $3 to $6 which makes Premium services more expensive than regular public transport but cheaper than taxis or private hire vehicles.

Types of Public Buses seen in SG

  • Single Deckers

    • Single Deckers

    Single Deckers are the most common type of public bus in SG with over 3,300 of them. These buses are usually 12 metres in length with 2 axles and 2 doors(newer models have 3 doors) with a single floor. Single deckers have a capacity of around 70 passengers at full load, suitable for routes with low to medium demand or frequent buses.

  • Bendy Buses

    • Bendy Buses

    Bendy buses(also known as articulated buses) in SG today are the rarest type of public bus with only 40 of them left. They can mostly be found in areas such as Choa Chu Kang, Woodlands and Yishun. The only bendy bus model in SG left is the MAN A24 bus, which is 18 metres long with 3 axles and 3 doors. These buses have a capacity of 130-131 passengers at full load, suitable for routes with high demand.

  • Double deckers

    • Double Deckers

    Double deckers in SG have become even more commonplace today due to many double decker buses being ordered to replace old bendy buses operated by SMRT as well as to increase fleet capacity. There are around 2,500 of them operating across the island today. These buses are usually 12 metres in length with 3 axles and 2 doors(newer models have 3 doors) with 2 floors. The bottom deck is able to carry both seating and standing passengers while the top deck is only able to carry seating passengers due to safety concerns and a low roof height. Double deckers have a capacity of around 139 passengers at full load, suitable for routes with high demand.

About SG's MRT system

Singapore's MRT system first opened in 1987 with a section of the North-South Line in the city. Today, there are 140 stations across 6 MRT lines and over 200km of track. The MRT system is operated by SMRT and SBS Transit. SMRT operates the North-South, East-West, Circle and Thomson-East Coast Lines while SBS Transit operates the North-East and Downtown Lines. The entirety of the MRT network is either elevated or underground with mobile data services across the entire network.

MRT Lines in SG

  • North-South Line

    • North-South Line

    The North-South Line(NSL) is the first MRT line to open in Singapore in 1987. The first section of the line to open was between Yio Chu Kang and Toa Payoh. Today, it runs 45 kilometres with 27 stations from Jurong East to Marina South Pier via Woodlands. Trains on the NSL run a 6-car formation with 4 doors per car. Since 2019, the NSL has upgraded its signalling system to have automatic train operations. Several other improvements have been made to the line since its opening, with platform screen doors being installed at overground stations, new stations like Canberra and introduction of new rolling stocks to increase capacity and replace older models.

  • East-West Line

    • East-West Line

    The East-West Line(EWL) is the second MRT line to be open in Singapore in 1987 from City Hall to Outram Park. Today, it is the longest MRT line in SG at 57.2 kilometres long with 35 stations from Pasir Ris to Tuas Link and a branch line from Tanah Merah to Changi Airport. Trains and equipment on the EWL are similar to that of the NSL and has fully automatic train operations as well. There used to be trains that ran direct from Boon Lay to Changi Airport but the Changi branch line was converted to a shuttle service due to low ridership. Today, trains run from Pasir Ris to Joo Koon, with every few westbound trains terminating at Tuas Link instead.

  • North-East Line

    • North-East Line

    The North-East Line(NEL) is SG's third MRT line, opening in 2003. At 20 kilometres long with 16 stations from Punggol to HarbourFront, it is SG's shortest MRT line. The line is famous for being the world's first fully-automated underground driverless heavy rail line. Trains on the NEL are powered by overhead wires and run 6 car formations with 4 doors per car. In terms of development, there is currently an extension from Punggol to Punggol Coast which is under construction and is expected to open in 2024. 2 stations - Buangkok and Woodleigh were opened in 2006 and 2011 respectively to serve the increased demand due to developments in the area. Stations on the line are wheelchair-accessible and most of them are Civil Defence shelters which can withstand airstrikes and chemical attacks.

  • Circle Line

    • Circle Line

    The Circle Line(CCL) is SG's fourth MRT line to be opened in 2009. It currently runs in an incomplete loop from Dhoby Ghaut to HarbourFront via Bishan and a branch to Marina Bay from Promenade. in 2026, the circle line will finally be a complete loop with the line being connected from HarbourFront to Marina Bay with 3 stations in between. The line is 35.5 kilometres long with 30 stations currently. The line is similar to that of the NEL in that it is both completely automated and fully underground. However, trains draw power from a third rail and are only 3 cars long with 4 doors each car.

  • Downtown Line

    • Downtown Line

    The Downtown Line(DTL) is SG's fifth MRT line with the first stage opening in 2013. As of today, it runs 41.9 kilometres from Bukit Panjang to Expo via the city area. It is fully automated and underground like the NEL and CCL and trains on the DTL run 3 car formations with 4 doors each car. There are currently plans to extend the line from Expo to connect it with the Thomson-East Coast line at Sungei Bedok and is expected to be completed in 2025. Hume station would also be opened by 2025 to serve Hillview residents and an extension from Bukit Panjang to Sungei Kadut(an upcoming station on the NSL) was announced to be completed by mid-2030s.

  • Thomson-East Coast Line

    • Thomson-East Coast Line

    The Thomson-East Coast Line(TEL) is the sixth MRT line in SG to be opened in 2020. Currently, it runs between Woodlands North and Gardens-by-the-Bay. When fully completed, the line will serve 32 stations over 43 kilometres from Woodlands North to Sungei Bedok. There are also plans for the line to merge with the existing Changi branch on the EWL. The trains on the TEL run 4 car formations with 5 doors per car, a first for SG. The TEL is a result of the merger between plans for a Thomson Line and the east coast stretch of the Eastern Region Line.
